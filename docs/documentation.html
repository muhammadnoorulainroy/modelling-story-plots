1. Overview and Design Principles

This ontology provides a formal knowledge model for describing story plots. It is designed around the Narn i Chîn Húrin (Tale of the Children of Húrin) by J.R.R. Tolkien but is general enough to apply to any fictional or historical narrative.

The design follows the separation of concerns principle: each aspect of the domain is modelled in a separate Turtle file (module). The modules are connected through owl:imports declarations and shared namespaces.

Key design decisions

  • Starting from existing ontologies: Three ontologies were given as starting points: meo.ttl (Middle-earth), fo.ttl (family relations), and rcc.ttl (RCC8 base). They are used as the base, with minimal repairs where needed (broken subPropertyOf links, misspelled OWL predicates, missing subClassOf declarations). The social, place, time, event, action, and plot modules were created from scratch.
  • Place vs. Region separation: A Place is a meaningful named location (Menegroth, Brethil); a Region is its abstract spatial extent. Places are linked to regions via loc:occupiesRegion, enabling RCC8 reasoning on places without conflating the two concepts.
  • Interior vs. tangential containment: Instead of a single partOf mapping to ntpp, the place module distinguishes interiorPartOf (maps to ntpp) and tangentialPartOf (maps to tpp) via separate property chains. This prevents places on the edge of a region (like Anfauglith on the border of Beleriand) from being forced into the wrong RCC8 relation.
  • Event vs. PlotEvent: An Event is something that happens in the fictional world (a battle, a birth). A PlotEvent is a narrative element that describes an event from the story-telling perspective. plot:describesEvent bridges the two layers.
  • Dual reality: The plot module distinguishes the real world (authors, editors, publishers, publication dates) from the fictional world (characters, in-world events). A NarrativeWork belongs to the real world; a Story belongs to the fictional world. plot:narrates is the bridge. This makes it impossible to accidentally compare real-world publication time with fictional story time.
  • Marriage with differentiated roles: Marriage events use hasHusband, hasWife, and hasOfficiant rather than generic hasSpouse1/hasSpouse2. The husband and wife roles carry rdfs:range fo:Male and fo:Female, so the reasoner infers gender from event participation. hasHusband and hasWife are declared owl:propertyDisjointWith each other.
  • Action as a defined class: Action ≡ Event ⊓ ∃hasAgent.⊤. Any event with an agent is automatically classified as an action. UseItemAction is also defined by equivalence (Action ⊓ ∃usesItem.Item), so the slaying of Glaurung is auto-classified as a UseItemAction because it uses Gurthang. AttackAction is kept as a conservative subclass because defining it purely by hasPatient would wrongly classify non-violent actions like curses as attacks.
  • Cross-module property chains: Property chains connect the event layer to the family and social layers. Birth events derive isAChildOf, death events derive killed, marriage events derive isMarriedTo, and residence events derive livedIn. The reasoner can discover family relations, social facts, and temporal ordering from event assertions alone.
  • Derived section ordering: plot:nextSection is inferred from endsWith ∘ nextPlotEvent ∘ startsWith⁻¹. Section adjacency follows from plot-event ordering rather than being asserted separately.
  • OWL 2 DL compatibility: Several axioms were constrained to stay within OWL 2 DL. Reflexive properties (rcc:eq, occursSimultaneouslyWith) cannot carry domain/range declarations without forcing every individual into a single class. Transitive and chain-derived properties cannot appear in propertyDisjointWith, AsymmetricProperty, or IrreflexiveProperty. Pairwise disjointness is declared only for simple properties. These tradeoffs are documented in the source file comments.
  • Rich axioms over broad coverage: The focus is on complex OWL axioms (cardinality restrictions, property chains, equivalence definitions, property disjointness, owl:hasKey, owl:AllDifferent, owl:AllDisjointClasses, owl:allValuesFrom with owl:unionOf) rather than a very large flat vocabulary.

2. Ontology Modules

2.1 Middle-earth Ontology (meo.ttl)

Namespace: http://purl.org/meo/
Source: Course material (Antoine Zimmermann)
Status: Included with minimal repairs

This ontology defines the races and peoples of Tolkien's legendarium: Ainur (Valar, Maiar), Eruhin (Elves/Quendi, Men/Atani), Dwarves (Casari/Naugrim), Trolls, Dragons (Lócë), and evil beings. It also contains rich axioms such as:

Axiom: "Whoever serves Morgoth is evil", expressed as [owl:Restriction on meo:served, owl:hasValue meo:morgoth] rdfs:subClassOf meo:EvilBeing.
Axiom: "Those whose parents are all Elves are also Elves". using owl:allValuesFrom on fo:isAChildOf.
Axiom: "Those with an Elvish parent and a Human parent are Half-elven (Peredhel)". using owl:intersectionOf with two someValuesFrom restrictions.

2.2 Family Ontology (fo.ttl)

Namespace: http://purl.org/family/
Source: Course material
Status: Included with minimal repairs

This ontology defines family relations (parent, child, sibling, spouse, ancestor, descendant) with gendered variants (father, mother, son, daughter, brother, sister). Notable features include:

  • fo:isACousinOf defined via property chain: isAChildOf ∘ isASiblingOf ∘ isAParentOf
  • fo:isAnAncestorOf is transitive and inverse of fo:isADescendentOf
  • fo:isMarriedTo is symmetric
  • Gender classes: fo:Male, fo:Female

2.5 Place Ontology (place.ttl)

Namespace: https://example.org/story/place#
Imports: rcc:

Places are organised in a hierarchy with disjointness axioms. Key class hierarchy:

Place
├── LandArea
│   ├── Settlement (disjoint with WildArea)
│   │   ├── City
│   │   ├── Town (disjoint with City, Hamlet)
│   │   ├── Hamlet
│   │   └── Fortress
│   ├── WildArea
│   │   ├── WoodedArea
│   │   │   └── Forest
│   │   ├── MountainRange
│   │   └── Plain
│   └── Realm
├── WaterArea (disjoint with LandArea)
│   ├── River (disjoint with Lake, Sea)
│   ├── Lake
│   └── Sea
├── Building
├── Room (⊑ ∃partOf.(Building ⊔ Fortress))
└── Road

Properties: loc:partOf (transitive; kept DL-safe without explicit asymmetry/irreflexivity), loc:interiorPartOf and loc:tangentialPartOf (specialised sub-properties of loc:partOf used to bridge place mereology to RCC8), loc:adjacentTo (symmetric, irreflexive), loc:occupiesRegion (functional, links to rcc:Region), loc:hasPopulation (on settlements only), loc:isCapitalOf (functional & inverse-functional, links Settlement → Realm).

Axiom: loc:isCapitalOf rdfs:subPropertyOf loc:partOf. A capital is necessarily a part of its realm.
Bridge axiom (property chain): (loc:occupiesRegion)⁻¹ ∘ loc:interiorPartOf ∘ loc:occupiesRegion ⊑ rcc:ntpp. If place P1 is an interior part of place P2, then the region of P1 is an ntpp of the region of P2. This formally connects Place-level mereology to RCC8 without collapsing tangential containment into non-tangential containment.
Bridge axiom (property chain): (loc:occupiesRegion)⁻¹ ∘ loc:tangentialPartOf ∘ loc:occupiesRegion ⊑ rcc:tpp. Tangential containment is tracked separately so border-touching places do not infer non-tangential proper-part relations.
Bridge axiom (property chain): (loc:occupiesRegion)⁻¹ ∘ loc:adjacentTo ∘ loc:occupiesRegion ⊑ rcc:ec. If two places are adjacent, their regions are externally connected.

2.6 Time Ontology (time.ttl)

Namespace: https://example.org/story/time#

This module defines instants, intervals, and Allen's 13 temporal interval relations. FiniteInterval is defined as the intersection of StartingInterval and EndingInterval (using owl:equivalentClass with owl:intersectionOf).

All 13 Allen relations are modelled with DL-safe OWL characteristics. Full pairwise owl:propertyDisjointWith axioms are documented rather than asserted because several relations are transitive or chain-derived. Property chain axioms connect instant-level ordering to interval-level relations:

Property chain: tm:endsAt ∘ tm:occursBefore ∘ (tm:startsAt)⁻¹ ⊑ tm:beforeInterval
Meaning: if the end-instant of interval I1 occurs before the start-instant of interval I2, then I1 is before I2.

2.7 Event Ontology (event.ttl)

Namespace: https://example.org/story/event#
Imports: loc:, tm:, fo:, soc:

The core of the knowledge model. Events are typed (Birth, Death, Marriage, Journey, Battle, War, Residence, Captivity, etc.) and have specialised participant roles with cardinality constraints.

Marriage event (differentiated roles)

Marriage events distinguish the husband, wife, and officiant rather than using generic "spouse1/spouse2":

evt:Marriage subClassOf (=1 hasHusband) and (=1 hasWife) and (<=1 hasOfficiant)

Event-level temporal relations (property chains)

Event-level relations (evt:beforeEvent, evt:meetsEvent, etc.) are derived from Allen interval relations via property chains through hasTemporalExtent:

evt:hasTemporalExtent ∘ tm:beforeInterval ∘ evt:isTemporalExtentOf ⊑ evt:beforeEvent

2.8 Action Ontology (action.ttl)

Namespace: https://example.org/story/action#
Imports: evt:, loc:

Actions are events with a deliberate agent. The class is defined using owl:equivalentClass:

act:Action ≡ evt:Event ⊓ ∃evt:hasAgent.⊤

This uses the general evt:hasAgent property (not act:hasAgent) so that any event asserting an agent, whether via evt:hasAgent directly or any sub-property, is automatically classified as an Action by a DL reasoner. act:UseItemAction is also defined by equivalence (Action and exists usesItem.Item), while AttackAction, MoveAction, SpeechAction, and CurseAction remain specialised subclasses. AttackAction is intentionally not defined purely by hasPatient, because that would wrongly classify non-violent actions such as curses as attacks.

Actions differ from mere events in having: (1) an intentional agent, (2) observable consequences (act:resultsIn).

2.9 Plot Structure (plot.ttl)

Namespace: https://example.org/story/plot#
Imports: evt:

This module handles the dual reality problem: the real world (where the author writes) and the fictional world (where characters act). The model separates:

  • Real world: plot:NarrativeWork (a book), plot:Author, plot:Editor, plot:Publisher, and publication metadata. These have real-world dates.
  • Fictional world: plot:Story, plot:StorySection (Prologue, Chapter, Epilogue), plot:PlotEvent. These have fictional timelines.
  • Bridge: plot:narrates connects NarrativeWork → Story; plot:describesEvent connects PlotEvent → Event.

This design makes it impossible to accidentally compare real-world publication time with fictional story time.

Section order is also partially derived rather than asserted manually: plot:nextSection is inferred from a section's end plot event, the next plot event in the narrative, and the following section's start plot event.

3. Instance File (narn-instances.ttl)

The instance file demonstrates all modules using concrete data from the Narn. It includes:

  • 12 characters typed using meo: classes (Túrin, Morwen, Húrin, Niënor, Galdor, Thingol, Glaurung, Mablung, Beleg, Morgoth, Finduilas, Orodreth)
  • Family relations using fo: (marriages, parent-child, siblings. 3 generations: Galdor, Húrin, Túrin)
  • Social relations using soc: (alliances, enmities, fostering, service, plus soc:enemyOf between Morgoth and Thingol to trigger the servant-of-my-enemy chain)
  • 10 places with specialised containment, capital relation, and adjacentTo (Beleriand → Doriath → Menegroth → Throne Room via loc:interiorPartOf; Menegroth loc:isCapitalOf Doriath; Anfauglith loc:tangentialPartOf Beleriand; Anfauglith adjacentTo Angband)
  • 10 spatial regions with RCC8 relations (ntpp, tpp, dc, ec)
  • 17 temporal instants with ordering, 18 intervals with Allen relations (before, meets, during, contains, finishes, equals), plus tm:occursDuring
  • 17 events (marriage, 2 births, battle, war, conversation, meeting, reign, 2 residences, 2 journeys, captivity, 4 deaths) and 2 actions (slaying, curse)
  • 2 items (Gurthang sword, Dragon-helm of Dor-lómin)
  • Action typing with the slaying of Glaurung asserted as act:AttackAction and inferred as act:UseItemAction, while Morgoth's curse is modeled as act:CurseAction
  • Plot structure with real-world book, 1 author, 1 editor, 1 publisher, 4 story sections, 9 plot events

Example: Answering CQ7: "What event ends story S?"

narn:NarnStory plot:endsWith narn:pe8 .
narn:pe8 plot:describesEvent narn:deathTurin .
→ The event that ends the Narn is the death of Túrin.

Example: Answering CQ4: "Did event E1 happen before E2?"

narn:marriageHurinMorwen evt:hasTemporalExtent narn:intMarriage .
narn:intMarriage tm:beforeInterval narn:intBirthTurin .       (asserted)
narn:birthTurin evt:hasTemporalExtent narn:intBirthTurin .
→ Via property chain (hasTemporalExtent ∘ beforeInterval ∘ isTemporalExtentOf):
  narn:marriageHurinMorwen evt:beforeEvent narn:birthTurin .  (inferred)

5. Competency Questions

The ontology supports the following competency questions:

CQ1: Who is parent of X? married to X? relative to X?
→ Answered via fo:isAParentOf, fo:isMarriedTo, and the general kinship properties in fo.ttl.
CQ2: Where did character X go during the story?
→ Via soc:livedIn for residences, and evt:Journey events with evt:hasOrigin / evt:hasDestination.
CQ3: Who was involved in event E?
→ Via evt:hasParticipant and its sub-properties.
CQ4: Did event E1 happen before event E2?
→ Via evt:beforeEvent (derived from Allen's tm:beforeInterval through property chains).
CQ5: Where did event E occur?
→ Via evt:hasLocation.
CQ6: Is it possible to be in location L1 and L2 at the same time?
→ Answered by combining temporal overlap with spatial separation: if two simultaneous presences are tied to regions related by rcc:dc, the query should flag them as incompatible. This is a query pattern over the OWL model, not a built-in contradiction under the Open World Assumption.
CQ7: What event ends story S?
→ Via plot:endsWith on the Story, then plot:describesEvent on the PlotEvent.
CQ8: What item was used as part of event E (or action A)?
→ Via evt:usesItem.
CQ9 (custom): Who was the officiant of a marriage?
→ Via evt:hasOfficiant on a Marriage event. The role is modelled explicitly even if the sample data leaves the officiant unspecified rather than inventing one.
CQ10 (custom): What is the consequence of action A?
→ Via act:resultsIn.
CQ11 (custom): Who fostered character X?
→ Via soc:fosteredBy.
CQ12 (custom): Is place P inside place Q?
→ Via loc:partOf, loc:interiorPartOf, or loc:tangentialPartOf at the place level, and via rcc:ntpp or rcc:tpp on their regions.
CQ13 (custom): Who contributes to the narrative work in the real-world layer?
→ Via plot:hasAuthor, plot:hasEditor, and plot:hasPublisher on a NarrativeWork.

7. AI Tool Usage

AI assistants (Claude, by Anthropic, and Codex) were used as discussion and review partners at different stages of the project. This section documents what was tried, what was rejected, and what design decisions came out of it.

6.1 The event/action distinction

The most substantive interaction was about the boundary between events and actions. The event/action distinction is quite conceptual, so using a chatbot as a sounding board seemed like a good approach.

What was asked

The initial prompt was: "In ontology engineering, what distinguishes an action from an event? Are actions a subclass of events, or an entirely separate category?"

What the chatbot proposed

The chatbot initially proposed a flat taxonomy where Action and Event were sibling classes under a shared superclass Occurrence. Its reasoning was that actions are "intentional" while events are "things that happen", and therefore they are ontologically distinct.

Why this was rejected

This proposal was rejected for three reasons:

  1. Redundancy: making Action a sibling of Event would require duplicating all temporal, spatial, and participant properties on both classes.
  2. Failure on the use case: in the Narn, the slaying of Glaurung is simultaneously an event (it happened at a place and time, it involved participants) and an action (Túrin deliberately performed it). A sibling taxonomy cannot express this without multiple inheritance hacks.
  3. Contradiction with established ontologies: the BBC Event Ontology, DOLCE, and schema.org all treat actions as a specialisation of events, not a parallel category.

The refined exchange

After pushing back, the chatbot was asked: "If actions are a subclass of events, what is the minimal differentia? What OWL construct best captures it?" The chatbot then suggested using owl:equivalentClass with an intersection: Action ≡ Event ⊓ ∃hasAgent.⊤. This was a significant improvement. It means any event with an agent is automatically classified as an action by a DL reasoner, without manual tagging. The chatbot also referenced Donald Davidson's 1963 essay "Actions, Reasons, and Causes" and the BDI (Belief-Desire-Intention) model from AI agent theory as philosophical grounding for the intentionality criterion.

What was adopted and what was modified

The equivalentClass pattern was adopted directly. It is elegant and leverages OWL 2 DL classification. However, the chatbot also suggested adding a hasIntention property (pointing to a MentalState class) to model the BDI aspect. This was rejected as over-engineering: modelling mental states would require a full philosophy-of-mind ontology that is far beyond the scope of story plot modelling. Instead, the simpler act:hasPurpose (a datatype property with a textual description) was used as a pragmatic alternative. The chatbot's suggestion of act:resultsIn as the second differentia of actions (actions have observable consequences) was adopted.

6.2 Plot structure and dual reality

A second interaction explored how to model the distinction between the real world (authors, publication) and the fictional world (characters, events). The chatbot was asked: "How should an ontology handle the fact that a story has both a real-world existence (a book written by an author) and an internal fictional reality (characters doing things)?"

The chatbot proposed a context/reification approach using named graphs (one graph per reality). While technically sound, this was rejected because:

  1. Named graphs are an RDF Dataset feature, not expressible in OWL 2 DL ontology files.
  2. A class-based separation (NarrativeWork vs Story) is simpler and directly expressible in Turtle.

The final design uses the class-based pattern with plot:narrates and plot:describesEvent as bridge properties. The chatbot confirmed that bridge properties (connecting the two layers without merging them) are a standard pattern in narrative ontologies.

6.3 Property chain design

The chatbot was consulted on whether the 3-hop property chain hasTemporalExtent ∘ beforeInterval ∘ isTemporalExtentOf ⊑ evt:beforeEvent would be valid in OWL 2 DL. It confirmed that OWL 2 supports property chains of arbitrary length in owl:propertyChainAxiom, subject to regularity constraints (the super-property must not appear in the chain). This was verified independently by checking the OWL 2 specification §9.2.1.

6.4 Repairs to the provided files

The provided ontologies were kept as the foundation of the project, but a few minimal repairs were necessary so that the combined ontology would remain loadable and semantically connected:

  • fo.ttl was repaired so that fo:isASonOf and fo:isADaughterOf correctly specialise fo:isAChildOf.
  • fo.ttl was also normalised around the sibling relation: fo:isASiblingOf is now the canonical property, while the misspelled fo:isASibingOf is preserved only as a deprecated backward-compatibility alias.
  • meo.ttl was minimally repaired by restoring missing subclass links for meo:Atan and correcting malformed OWL vocabulary such as owl:inverseOf.
  • These repairs are deliberately narrow: they preserve the teacher-provided modules as the basis of the work while making them usable in a coherent OWL 2 ontology.

The goal of these edits was not to replace the provided ontologies, but to keep them usable as course material inside a reasoner-ready modular project.

Later consistency review and debugging were assisted by Codex. Its role was adversarial rather than generative: challenge OWL 2 DL legality, look for unintended inferences, spot stale documentation claims, and rerun parsing and HermiT checks after each repair. Suggestions were kept only when they matched the source ontology and reasoner behaviour.
